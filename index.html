About Armenia

There is a land where history is measured not in centuries but in millennia. Where monasteries cling to cliffs as they have for 1,700 years. Where the world's oldest winery sleeps in a cave, and the world's oldest leather shoe was preserved in the same cool darkness. This is Armenia.

Tucked between the peaks of the Caucasus and the high plateau that bears its name, Armenia is one of civilization's oldest continuous cultures. The Armenian alphabet, created in 405 AD, still graces the pages of ancient manuscripts kept in Yerevan's Matenadaran‚Äîone of the world's great repositories of medieval knowledge. The people here have been making wine for over 6,000 years, carving churches from living rock, and welcoming strangers as honored guests since before Rome was founded.

In 301 AD, Armenia became the first nation on earth to adopt Christianity as its state religion. Today, more than 4,000 churches and monasteries dot a landscape of impossible beauty‚Äîsnow-capped Mount Ararat rising on the horizon, Lake Sevan shimmering at nearly 2,000 meters altitude, and gorges of red rock sheltering medieval treasures. The cuisine here is ancient too: pomegranates, apricots (which likely originated here), fresh herbs, grilled meats, and lavash bread baked in clay ovens as it has been for centuries.

But what visitors remember most is not the monuments or the food‚Äîit's the people. Armenians possess a warmth that goes beyond hospitality. Invite a stranger to dinner and watch as the table fills with dishes, the glasses with wine and toasts, the room with laughter. This generosity of spirit, forged through millennia of history, is Armenia's greatest treasure.

A Brief History

Armenia's story begins in the mists of antiquity. The Kingdom of Urartu flourished here around 860 BC, leaving behind fortress walls and bronze artifacts. When that civilization faded, the Armenian people emerged, their identity crystallizing around a unique language, alphabet, and eventually, faith.

The adoption of Christianity in 301 AD‚Äîa full decade before the Roman Empire‚Äîmade Armenia the world's first Christian nation. The centuries that followed saw the creation of the Armenian alphabet by the monk Mesrop Mashtots, an explosion of literature and learning, and the construction of the monasteries that still define the landscape. Despite centuries of foreign rule‚Äîby Persians, Arabs, Mongols, Ottomans, and Soviets‚ÄîArmenian culture not only survived but flourished in diaspora communities from Jerusalem to Venice to Los Angeles.

Modern Armenia, independent since 1991, is a nation of three million with a diaspora of ten million more scattered across the globe. Today, Yerevan buzzes with cafes, galleries, and a food scene that's capturing international attention‚Äîyet drive thirty minutes in any direction and you're in a landscape unchanged for centuries, where shepherds tend flocks beneath medieval towers and monasteries echo with ancient hymns.

The Discovery

The World's Oldest Winery: Areni-1 Cave

In 2007, archaeologists made a discovery that would rewrite the history of winemaking. Deep within the Areni-1 cave in Armenia's Vayots Dzor province, they uncovered a complete 6,100-year-old winery‚Äîa millennium older than any previously known.

The cave revealed a wine press, fermentation vat, storage jars, grape seeds, and dried grape vines. "For the first time, we have a complete archaeological picture of wine production," declared UCLA archaeologist Gregory Areshian.

The cave's stable microclimate preserved everything remarkably well. Nearby burials with drinking cups suggest the wine was used in funeral rituals‚Äîperhaps humanity's oldest toast to the departed.

The World's Oldest Leather Shoe

5,500 years old

In 2008, archaeologists discovered a perfectly preserved leather moccasin in the Areni-1 cave‚Äî1,000 years older than the Great Pyramid of Giza. The shoe, stuffed with grass (perhaps as insulation), is now displayed at the History Museum of Armenia.

"I was amazed to find that even the shoe-laces were preserved," recalled PhD student Diana Zardaryan, who made the discovery.

The World's Oldest Winery

6,100 years old

The Areni-1 cave also yielded the world's oldest complete winery‚Äîa wine press, fermentation vat, storage jars, and grape remains. This discovery pushed back the history of winemaking by over 1,000 years.

"This is the earliest, most reliable evidence of wine production," declared UCLA archaeologist Gregory Areshian.

First Christian Nation

301 AD

Armenia became the first nation to adopt Christianity as its state religion in 301 AD‚Äîa decade before Rome's Edict of Milan. Gregory the Illuminator, imprisoned for 13 years in a pit at Khor Virap, eventually converted King Tiridates III.

Today, more than 4,000 monasteries and churches dot the Armenian landscape.

Mount Ararat & Noah's Ark

Biblical tradition

According to the Book of Genesis, Noah's Ark came to rest on Mount Ararat after the great flood. While the mountain now lies in Turkey, it remains the sacred symbol of Armenia, visible from Yerevan and appearing on the national coat of arms.

Armenians often say they are descendants of Noah through his great-great-grandson Hayk.
